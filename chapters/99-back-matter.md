## Acknowledgments

This book grows out of INFO 5100 and the students who make the course real:
students who arrive with different programming backgrounds, different
professional goals, and different levels of confidence, but who share the
difficult work of turning abstract programming ideas into running applications.
We are grateful to the Northeastern University College of Engineering, to the
EDGE learning community, and to the many colleagues, teaching assistants,
course builders, and reviewers whose questions sharpened the course design.

We are also grateful to the students working in and around AI-assisted
education projects, including Medhavy, Humanitarians AI, and related learning
tool efforts. Their work keeps returning us to the same question: what should a
human learner become better at when powerful tools become easier to use?

## About the Authors

**Nik Bear Brown** is an Associate Teaching Professor in the Northeastern
University College of Engineering. He teaches and builds courses in artificial
intelligence, machine learning, programming, data science, visualization,
virtual environments, and AI fluency. His background includes a PhD and MS in
Computer Science from UCLA, an MS in Information Design and Data Visualization,
an MBA from Northeastern University, and a BA in Biochemistry and Molecular
Biology from UC Santa Cruz. His work spans AI education infrastructure,
computational skepticism, data visualization, computational biology, music
technology, and production systems for AI-assisted learning.

Brown is the founder of Humanitarians AI and Bear Brown & Company, and he has
led or contributed to educational tools including Medhavy, DevProof, Jobsekr,
CRITIQ, SOCRIT, Botspeak, and the Brutalist system for AI-assisted creative
production. Across these projects, his central interest is the same one that
runs through this book: AI can extend human capability, but only when a human
can specify, test, revise, and take responsibility for the work.

**Khaled Bugrara** is an Executive Director in the Northeastern University
College of Engineering and a long-standing leader in information systems and
computing education. He earned his PhD in Computer Science from Indiana
University Bloomington, where his doctoral work focused on analysis of
backtracking search algorithms. His academic formation included work in
algorithms, satisfiability testing, and questions about machine cognition and
computer thinking.

Bugrara's work at Northeastern has connected computer science, information
systems, program leadership, and applied software projects. His projects have
included tablet-based clinical and transport-medicine systems, mobile EHR
platforms, and real-world application environments where software design must
serve people under operational constraints. That applied orientation shapes the
course: students are not learning Java as an isolated syntax exercise. They are
learning how to design applications that carry responsibility in the world.

## No Index

This edition is designed for Kindle, online reading, and integration with
Medhavy. A traditional back-of-book index is less useful in those environments
than search, links, glossary entries, and adaptive navigation. Kindle readers
can search the full text directly, and Medhavy can connect terms, modules,
examples, and review questions dynamically.

Medhavy, also written Medhavi, takes its name from मेधावी: a Sanskrit word
meaning "intelligent" or "intellectually brilliant." The goal is an
AI-powered intelligent textbook system that helps learners move through a book
with context, practice, hints, and feedback while preserving the human work of
struggle, explanation, and judgment.

## Glossary

**abstract class** — A class that cannot be instantiated directly and may
contain abstract methods that subclasses must implement.

**AI phase gate** — A course boundary that defines what AI may do in a module
and what the student must still do and verify.

**array** — A fixed-size indexed container for values of the same type.

**class** — A Java template that defines the fields and methods shared by a
kind of object.

**collection** — A Java structure for storing groups of objects, such as a
list, set, queue, or map.

**constructor** — A special method-like block that initializes a new object.

**encapsulation** — The practice of keeping an object's state behind methods
that protect its invariants.

**event handler** — Code registered to respond when a GUI event occurs.

**GUI** — A graphical user interface made of visible controls, layout
containers, and event responses.

**inheritance** — A relationship in which one class extends another and
inherits its behavior and state structure.

**interface** — A Java contract that specifies behavior a class promises to
provide.

**method** — A named block of behavior that can receive inputs, perform work,
and optionally return a value.

**object** — A specific runtime instance of a class with its own identity and
state.

**polymorphism** — The ability to treat different object types through a shared
supertype while allowing runtime behavior to vary by actual object type.

**recursion** — A problem-solving structure in which a method calls itself on a
smaller or simpler version of the problem.

**verification** — The process of gathering evidence that code behaves as the
requirement demands, not merely that it compiles or appears plausible.
